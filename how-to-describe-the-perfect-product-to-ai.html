How to Describe the Perfect Product to AI

Learn how to turn a vague shopping idea into a clear AI product search that finds products matching your real needs.

In this article

1. Start with the situation, not the product 2. Separate must-haves from preferences 3. Use limits that reflect reality 4. Describe what you dislike 5. Let the AI explain the match

The first time I used AI to help me shop, I made the same mistake almost everyone makes: I asked for “a good one.” A good laptop. A good chair. A good gift. The answer sounded useful, but it was still generic. The breakthrough came when I stopped naming product categories and started describing the life around the product.

Start with the situation, not the product

Tell the AI what is happening in your life. Are you commuting every day? Buying for a small apartment? Choosing something for a child who tends to drop things? Context changes what “best” means. A lightweight laptop may be perfect for a student and frustrating for a video editor. A compact coffee machine may be ideal in a studio and annoying in a large household.

Separate must-haves from preferences

I like to divide a request into three layers: requirements, strong preferences, and nice extras. Requirements are non-negotiable. Preferences matter, but can bend. Extras are bonuses. This prevents the search from becoming either too broad or impossibly strict.

Use limits that reflect reality

Add your maximum budget, acceptable size, delivery region, compatibility needs, and anything you refuse to compromise on. Saying “under €100” is useful. Saying “as cheap as possible” is not, because it gives the AI no meaningful boundary.

Describe what you dislike

Negative preferences are surprisingly powerful. Mention that you do not want a subscription, glossy plastic, loud fans, proprietary refills, a gamer aesthetic, or a product that requires an app. Avoiding the wrong products is often more valuable than finding another almost-right option.

Let the AI explain the match

Ask why each product fits. A recommendation becomes much easier to trust when you can see the connection between your request and the result. The explanation also helps you notice whether your own priorities were clear.

Try a request like this

“I need a compact mechanical keyboard for writing and occasional gaming. It must be quiet enough for a shared office, work with Windows and Mac, have wireless and wired modes, and cost under €120. I prefer a clean design without bright gamer styling. A volume knob would be nice, but it is not essential.”
