Find Products by Description

Find products even when you only know what they should look like, feel like, or help you do.

In this article

1. You do not need the official category 2. Sensory details matter 3. Explain the moment of use 4. Add examples without becoming trapped by them 5. Refine after the first search

Some of the hardest products to find are the ones you can picture perfectly but cannot name. You know the shape, the feeling, the problem it solves, maybe even the color—but not the search term. This is exactly where description-based shopping shines.

You do not need the official category

Retail categories are created for inventory systems, not human thought. You might describe “a small lamp that clips to a headboard and does not wake my partner.” That is enough to begin, even if the store calls it a rechargeable dimmable reading light.

Sensory details matter

Words such as soft, matte, quiet, sturdy, warm, minimal, flexible, and lightweight carry real information. They help the AI infer materials, construction, and design choices.

Explain the moment of use

Describe where the product will live and how it will be used. A speaker for a bathroom has different needs from one for a garden party. A bag for cycling in rain is different from a bag for occasional train travel.

Add examples without becoming trapped by them

You can say “something with the simplicity of X, but cheaper and smaller.” Examples give direction, but your actual requirements should remain more important than the reference brand.

Refine after the first search

The first result can teach you the vocabulary you were missing. Use that new language to sharpen the next request. Description-based search works best as a conversation.

Try a request like this

“I am looking for a narrow piece of furniture beside my sofa. It should hold a drink and a book, slide partly under the couch, and look warm rather than industrial. Dark wood or walnut preferred.”
